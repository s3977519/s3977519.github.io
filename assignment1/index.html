Assignment 1 Review

Introduction

The Frank Gehry: Walt Disney Concert Hall Website

The Gehry Getty webpage is an interactive narrative that describes how Frank Gehry and his team designed the Walt Disney Concert hall. The webpage documents the design processes, ideas, inspirations, and various 3D models, of the concert hall, This website created an engaging experience through featuring interactive elements, including narrations by Frank Gehry, and music performed by LA Phil that worked in sync with the digital graphics, and throughout the webpage.

Week 2

The first thing I noted was the logo and the word “initialising” that popped up on the loading screen, diverting my attention to the animated sketch lines. The instructions “click anywhere to enable sound”, which was its suggestion for the best experience, flowed in right before the landing page fully loaded, giving contextual clues to the user on what kind of media content they would be experiencing. In this case, it is one where sound would be involved.

The landing page presents sketch graphics, a large type element — animated typography, the title description of the content of the web — “How Frank Gehry and his team designed a monument to music — the story…” and a caption instructing how to navigate — “scroll for story”

The following list describes the actions/steps in my experience:

  1. Clicking on the “menu” button (determine webpage’s spread of content and accessibility)
  2. Hovering over design elements (check for buttons)
  3. Scrolling down as instructed
  4. Conducting a quick scroll without reading any of the content (gain quick understanding of the flow and navigation of the webpage)
  5. Scroll back to the top to read actual content

The music and the animated large typography headers were the most engaging elements in the website. The music was an ever present engagement throughout the experience. It played through the video monologues, and whilst gliding through and reading the content.

Following that would be the large text headers that acted as a break between content. The text would stretch and squash when scrolled past, which created for a smooth and bouncy rhythmic transition that synced in time with the background music. It was not until the third time going through the webpage where I discovered that when you clicked and hold, there were hidden elements within the transition header.

In the first header “sculpting harmony”, there were hidden text and sentences that would be unveiled as you hover over them. Sentences would appear word by word following the direction of your mouse. And in the second and a few others, the sketch artwork behind the text moves in response to your mouse.

The main two actions I primarily used to engage with the webpage was a mix of hovering for clickable items and scrolling. The goal was to note where and what should be relooked into when going through the actual content of the page, to avoid missing out any relevant or key pieces of information that was intended from the experience of the website.

The interactive experience aimed to create and inform audiences a detailed and information-packed story of the creative processes behind designing and building behind the Walt Disney Concert Hall in an engaging way — as if physically attending a museum exhibition of the display, a first-hand experience.

Through the use of interactive and dynamic elements within the design layout and structure, the emphasis on fluidity and movement showed intentionality in bringing out the theatrical and musical experience that is the subject — The Concert Hall.

The interactive experience documented and curated significant details on the subject. It is arranged in chapters which draws the connection to the purpose of the webpage being a storytelling experience.

The chapters begin with an overall introduction, followed by the vision and inspirations of the architect. It goes on to reveal the context of his intentions behind designing specific features/design choices made, and the following steps and processes to achieve the design — documenting multiple views and images of the hall, design footprints and wireframes, digital sketches and renders, annotations, photographs of the physical workspace where they worked on the 3D models, photographs of them working on the model, and finally, to the finished execution.

Through the the process of displaying these contents, supplementary design elements, such as the transitions — (text headings, reveal of texts upon scroll, videos between text, transition from new chapter introduction to full chapter content, a more organic dynamic grid layout of images and text) between each chapter, within the chapter and throughout the webpage worked to accentuate the grand design and function of the MUSIC CONCERT Hall.

The typical experience should be interacted with in 3-4 different ways at varying spans of time, totaling to an estimate of 1 hour to 1 hour and 15 minutes.

The first time going over the webpage, I mainly focused on the navigation, to gain a quick understanding of the layout and flow of the content. This was a really swift process spanning about a minute or so.

Based on the first encounter with the web, I understood that the primary action I had to take was to scroll in order for the content to load. There were no external links involved, just scrolling and clicking on “read more” or “explore the model” buttons which remained on the same page.

Thus, going over the webpage a second time, I focused more on uncovering the hidden elements that were within the page. Looking through design elements, hovering over and letting the animations play and or transitions play out at a natural pace to determine if they hid and or revealed further details in relevance to the content. This process took an estimated time of 5 minutes.

Finally, going over the webpage a third time, I would then dive into the actual content that is presented. This process would take a significant period of time, depending on the amount of content, viewers’ reading speed, anywhere from 30 minutes to 1 hour or more.

Additionally, if the viewer enjoyed the content and experience, they would be going over the webpage for the fourth time. This is where the process of playing with the design elements could/would be repeated, as the experience was pleasurable and would leave them to linger for a couple of minutes more.

However, the webpage is also designed in a way for easy use as a study reference that could be gone over and over again, jumping and honing on specific details and chapters of the story.

The simple scroll down structure of the webpage creates a simple and accessible experience for users.

The “menu” button features chapters that allows for users to skip to specific parts of the story, thus enabling easy revisitation of information, giving ease for users to re-read content.

The “read more" button expands the section on the page instead of creating an external link or loading on a separate page, which makes the design a more enjoyable, simpler and more convenient experience, aiding a smoother flow to reading and following the story.

Lastly, the page does not reload and refresh upon switching tabs which allows one to read where they left off.

Week 3

The webpage draws parallel to a cinematic film or trailer, documentary. The introduction section opens with cinematic angles of the concert hall with text animating in, mimicking subtitles, use of motion and smooth transitions from one section to another — more text animation transitions, and enlarging and minimising of elements while scrolling which similarly acts like a “zoom-in” motion on camera, video clips embedded in between, use of audio)

In addition, the webpage also resembles the physical experience of visiting a museum exhibition. The overall experience involved the processes of interactive self-discovery and engagement of multiple senses which was created by the embedded motion and sound within the webpage. In contrast to a static infographic pamphlet, it is thus similar to physically walking around in a museum, to read the descriptions noted on the side of the display, with sound and music playing in the exhibition hall, and to walk up to sections of clips and videos of interviews. Lastly, as mentioned, it is also structured like a book, with an introduction, chapters – perhaps a reference study, research book/journal.

The research informative nature of the story — one of a densely packed informative piece, revealed several design elements that significantly helped create a more user friendly experience. As mentioned above, transitions between each chapter of the story, music, animated elements were all relevant to increase immersive sensorial experience.

These were intentional design elements within the structure of the layout that aided the ease of reading. For example, the layout structure broke up potentially long and large paragraphs of text into sections. The typical chapter starts off with the boldly typed header and coloured background. It then flows and transits into an introductory body of text that is laid on the bold background colour, which enlarges slowly to fullscreen introducing the main content chapters, signalling and indicating a call for increased focus and attention to subsequent content.

Within the layout of the chapters itself, the overall deliberation of the design led to a highly engaging piece that mimicked the experience of motion, i.e. watching a video, to an otherwise static piece of information.

The grid layout of the webpage was more organic. It had varying sizes of images and ratio placements, the text and photograph arrangements had users’ gaze moving from left to right across the screen in digestible portions which effectively sought to increase engagement and decrease weariness while reading. The story chapters were also arranged and displayed with mixed mediums, showcasing videos, interactive 3D models which added to breaking up the monotony of content-heavy information.

Typically, facts and information are conveyed in a way that does not necessarily resonate with the reader, it involves a one-dimensional, static, no sensorial engagement sort of reading, its experience is like hearing about something from someone, a secondary source. However, in this web design, it essentially presented a personal, and immersive first-hand encounter with the content by creating relatability and relevance through interactive and motion elements.

Due to the nature of the webpage, scrolling is a key navigating action. The scrolling process in the introduction was smooth and consistent in its reveal of a new text section, conveying clear indications of landing on new content, which made it easy.

However, this was not consistent through the webpage which made it annoying as the users would have to adjust their strength of scrolling between chapters, disrupting the smooth rhythmic flow that was designed and communicated to users in the unveiling of the story.

For example, the transition from a header text to the actual text content portions within the chapter, had a significant difference in the strengths of scrolling. To scroll past the header smoothly, one would scroll a lot further than within the content. That initial transition when scrolling over the header leads to skipping over the first paragraph or section of the content, resulting in an extra effort to scroll back up and then adjust the speed of scrolling. Preferably, the scroll past the header should glide to land right at the start of the content paragraph, then allow users to adjust their speed after, and scroll through text heavy information slowly and at their own pace.

The most satisfying part of the interaction was ultimately these dynamic movements within the layout of the webpage, especially in the beginning and nearing the end header text animations, “SCULPTING HARMONY” and “BOUQUET OF SOUND”. The bold text “SCULPTING HARMONY” was responsible for creating a first impression.

To top it all off, the introductory and final credit display of the models, rotating the angles and zoom in and or out really created an impactful cinematic experience of the conclusion and start of the subject at study. Each zoom sequence and transition was rhythmic, dramatic and theatrical, matching up with the music and heightened with animated text reveals floating in like subtitle/caption sequences. The design effectively brought forth the concert and film-like experience.
